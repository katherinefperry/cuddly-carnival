Dignity, food, and why it matters.

“It’s not just about feeding people. This is about social inclusion, teaching people about food waste and giving hope to people who have lost all hope.” (Massimo Boturra)

Posted by Kati Perry on August 15, 2016

Given my unwavering love for fresh bread, red wine, and all good food everywhere, con pan, meaning "with bread," suits this blog perfectly. More crucially, however, con pan forms the roots of integral Spanish words that refer to friends and neighbors, like compañero, acompañar, and compañía, according to the Real Academia Española.

The words con, meaning "with", and pan meaning "bread", work together to indicate that breaking and sharing bread with others means something significant for humans — so much so that it underpins the language we use to refer to one another. And the Spanish language hints at the social element of eating in more ways than one. For example, sobremesa one that doesn't exist in English, refers to the conversation that ensues after a meal is finished.

The value of eating with another person and the resulting nourishment, both physical and mental, cannot be overstated. The way we eat is a matter of human dignity, as every person not only deserves nutritious meals, but also the fulfillment that we derive from eating together.

This idea inspired my undergraduate thesis and also inspires my current research, which seeks to understand the way those without homes, families and communities nourish themselves emotionally and physiologically. In Spain, where eating is an inherently social activity and tapas dominate menus, such a study is especially pertinent.

The way that Teresa Barrenchea, author of The Cuisines of Spain: Exploring Regional Home Cooking," describes tapas points to the highly social element of the custom in Spain. She commented that "the term tapas [has] come to imply the act of going out," and that "after completing the first round of lively conversation and small plates, the group moves on to the next bar." Such a practice is as culturally crucial as it is fulfilling, but it leaves those individuals without a community or family at a loss.

That loss is the subject of my study in Spain. By working in food kitchens in Madrid, a burgeoning metropolitan center with more than 6 million people, I hope to learn more about how impoverished communities fill that human need for healthy social interaction in a country where food serves as a primary conductor.
